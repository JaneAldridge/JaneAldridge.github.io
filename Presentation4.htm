•      We selected Software Supply Chain Management as being the biggest trend over the next 5 years, because of the impact of industry 4.0 which will increase the risk of the software supply chain, as companies become more reliant on external vendors

•      Geer et al (2020) call this trend to centralise on certain technology services as ‘market concentration’ and they refer to the interconnection which this brings, in terms of cyber security risk

•      Market concentration is about size, scale, centralization and interconnectedness (Geer et al, 2020)

•      Examples of concentration range from operating systems (Microsoft Windows, Apple OS), protocols (e.g. WPA2, WiFi), content delivery networks (e.g. Cloudfare, Akamai), cloud computing services (e.g. AWS, Azure), anti-virus vendors (e.g. Symantec, ESET), aggregation platforms (e.g. Reddit) etc.. (Geer et al, 2020)

•      These large organisations, which are acting as ‘hubs’ due to market concentration are a magnet for malicious activity

•      They are also highly interconnected, via data sharing agreements, common supply chains and third party vendors (Geer et al, 2020)

•      What happens to big organisations, such as Oracle, ripples out and effects others

•      The risk of a cyber attack is transferred to bigger players in the market

•      As an example the migration to the cloud has meant that organisations are dependent on a third party for their data and infrastructure

•      The Solarwinds incident shows the impact that 3rd party software can have

•      Industry 4.0 causes organisations to have more external risk, and there are a lack of risk methods which focus on external risk

•      As an example CVSS is very inward looking, it wouldn’t take into consideration the external risk of open source components or third party software and yet it is the most widespread risk methodology (Spring et al, 2021)

•      There needs to be transparency in the software supply chain so that risk can be quantified

•      There needs to be predictive cyber defence for supply chain management as a result of industry 4.0

•      Other areas outside of cyber security are already using predictive analytics to predict suspicious behaviour, such as Compliance monitoring systems within banking, where algorithms have been built to predict certain patterns in trading

•      Machine Learning and AI can be used to for predictive cyber risk analytics (Radanliev et al, 2020)

•      Radanliev et al also propose that raltionships in supply chains, can be visualised so that technical risk for a given vulnerability can be better assessed.

•      The aim is to enable predictive risk analytics, which would need to be in real time and have access to the data within the supply chain for monitoring and predicting cyber security attacks
