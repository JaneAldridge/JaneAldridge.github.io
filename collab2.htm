Time to Change the CVSS - What characteristics of CVSS do the authors (Spring et al, 2021) criticise?

 

1)      The Lack of Justification for the CVSS Formula

 

The authors state that a major issue with the calculation of CVSS scores, is that to calculate a base score, eight questions have to be answered, and these questions are based on qualitative data. These answers are then converted into a scale between 0 and 10.  The CVSS v3.0 equation was based on data from experts who ranked 100 vulnerabilities.  So the basis of the data is qualitative.

 

The authors also make the point that the CVSS documentation is not transparent about how the vulnerability score is calculated, and that qualitative measurements should not be converted into numbers.

 

I agree that CVSS relies solely on the judgement of human assessors, and so this is subjective, even if an assessor is off by several points on a 10 point scale, it will alter the scoring significantly (Campagna, 2020).

 

2)      Operational Problems with Scoring

 

The authors also highlight the problems with assigning scoring, in terms of inconsistencies and vague guidelines. To demonstrate this the authors carried out a survey and found that 50% of experienced security professionals surveyed mis-scored vulnerabilities using CVSS (Spring et al, 2021).

 

In addition, the authors state that CVSS defaults to the assumption of ´assume the worst´, in terms of the impact of a vulnerability.  CVSS does not use public repositories of exploits, such as Metaspoilt or exploitDB, which would provide data to refine the impact of a vulnerability.   

 

In fact, CVSS needs to correlate data from a number of sources (Aboud, 2020), which would include threat and exploit intelligence data.  In addition, CVSS needs to include an assessment of current and predicted future attacker activity, as well as taking into consideration the age of a vulnerability, its potential for harm, the degree to which it is exploitable and how frequently the threat is being seen (Aboud, 2020)

 

3)      CVSS scores severity, not security risk

 

The authors state that CVSS categorises vulnerabilities in terms of severity, that severity is then used to prioritise vulnerabilities in terms of remediation (Spring et al, 2021), but that using CVSS as a risk score is not correct, although many organisations do use CVSS directly as a risk score. As examples they refer to the US government and the payment card industry. The article not only states that CVSS does not support risk scoring, but it goes further and states that ´it fails to inform vulnerability management´.

 

I think an important point here is the difference between vulnerability scoring and risk.  Vulnerability refers to a weakness in hardware, software and procedures, whereas risk is the potential for lost, damaged or destroyed assets (Glover, 2022). So risk must take into account the probability of an event happening, whereas vulnerability scoring will not.  Risk also needs to look at the broader context, so as an example, rather than looking at a risk in isolation there could be a chain of vulnerabilities which could increase the risk impact and also the probability of an event occurring.  Spring et al, also make this point that context and the chain of potential events needs to be taken into account, which CVSS does not do :

 

´CVSS does not account, broadly for context….. related or chained vulnerabilities…. and different interpretations of a CVSS score in different communities´

 

The authors state that organisations want to know the risk of a vulnerability and the priority of a vulnerability, in terms of how quickly they should respond and remediate a vulnerability.

 

I agree that CVSS should not be used as a risk score.  Gunter Ollman (September 2019) states that CVSS should never be used as a risk score, and that it is an important variable in the risk calculation but is not adequate to calculate risk in itself.  Abby Ross (2019) also backs up this view, that CVSS is being misused as a risk score.

 

4)      Failure to Account for Context

 

4.1) Technical Context

 

The authors state that there are various ways that CVSS does not take into account context, firstly in terms of the technical context :

 

CVSS does not capture the complexity of shared libraries, for example, where specific functions are being called

 Vulnerabilities can be chained together, increasing risk, although CVSS v3.0 provides some guidance about vulnerability chaining it isn’t sufficient

Web vulnerabilities are not taken into account

CVSS uses technical severities of low, medium, high, critical but there are no definitions for these scores, and also the definitions could change depending on the organisational context

Gunter Ollman (September 2019) states that CVSS was intended to score vulnerabilities in the context of software and devices, but not from a system context, which supports the view that CVSS does not support chained vulnerabilities or even system level vulnerabilities.  CVSS does not consider the relationship between vulnerabilities, as an example CVSS would not link the ability of an attacker to escalate privileges and then attack insecure misconfigurations (Ross, 2019)

 

4.2) Organisational Context

 

A really important point that the authors make is that CVSS gives equal waiting to vulnerabilities in terms of confidentiality, integrity and availability.

 

However, the weighting will depend on the context, as an example data loss, in terms of confidentiality is going to be more important to a regulated financial institution and availability is going to be more important for embedded devices medical devices, like pace makers.  The authors do make the point that CVSS v3.1 provides an option for adjusting the importance of confidentiality, integrity and availability, but state that because the adjustment is part of the CVSS formula it is not transparent and so it cannot be determined if this is an adequate solution.  

 

The CVSS score for a bank should be different for an Oil company like BP or Shell, even if the vulnerabilities were the same.  Gunter Ollman states that every industry assesses risk differently and each business has its own risk factor qualification which an external party won´t know. CVSS does not take into account the bigger risk picture and is just one component of the risk picture (Ross, 2019). CVSS also does not take into consideration the value of an exposed asset to an organisation (Ross, 2019).

 

5)      Failure to Account for Consequences

 

The authors state that the score should include the consequences of an exploitation.  CVSS 3.0 tries to address the likelihood of a vulnerability being exploited, but assumes widespread exploitation. The authors further make the point that high CVSS scores are not correlated to whether a vulnerability will be exploited.

 

I agree that CVSS needs to take into account the potential for exploitation and also the criticality of an asset (Ross, 2019).

 

6)      Not all Vulnerabilities are Captured

 

The authors also make the point that CVSS is not going to capture threats such as misconfigurations, incorrect default settings, and the threats that arise from the integration of systems.

 

7)      An Alternative – SSVC

 

SSVC (Stakeholder- Specific Vulnerability Categorization) is an alternative to CVSS proposed by Spring et al.  In my view this methodology is a better alternative, due to the following reasons, which try to address the issues with CVSS listed previously:

 

7.1) One of the key advantages of SSVC is that it tries to avoid a ‘one-size-fits-all solution’ (Spring et al, 2019). I think this is an important point, organisations are complex as are the systems which support them, and all organisations are different and none of them will have the same vulnerabilities. I think that CVSS takes an overly simplistic approach to capturing vulnerabilities. SSVC focuses on the decision making aspect of vulnerability management, which is the ultimate outcome of CVSS, to make decisions about priorities and where to focus resources.  SSVC uses a framework which identifies the inputs, determines the aspects of vulnerability management which are in scope, and also defines the context (Spring et al, 2019).  SSVC tries to capture the fact that all organisations are different, and also it aims to deal with organisational complexity.

 

 7.2) SSVC uses decision trees to support the decision making process. The decision tree shows the important elements of a decision, possible decision values and possible outcomes.  The decision trees are used to provide advice about vulnerability prioritization and are easily understood by stakeholders. CVSS uses technical severity as the key factor, not the decision which is the ultimate outcome. Some of the decision points will require substantial analysis but this is to be expected, because analysing vulnerabilities in any organisation is going to take time and multiple iterations.

 

 7.3)   SSVC states that it is as qualitative approach, it doesn’t provide a ‘false sense of security’ by using numerical representations.  Unless there is a source(s) of data which is actually proven for all vulnerabilities and different organisations, then a numerical representation is too simplistic.

 

‘Each vulnerability has a potentially unique relationship to the socio-technical system in which it exists, including the internet.  The context of the vulnerability, and the systems it impacts, are inextricably linked to managing it. Temporal and environmental considerations should be primary’ (Spring et al, 2019)

 

Organisations are too complex and each has a different environment to use a purely quantitative approach. Industry 4.0 increases complexity and causes a greater shift of risk to external vulnerabilities, which are external to an organisation.

 

7.4) SSVC involves key stakeholders in the decision modelling process, and this makes sense, because these stakeholders will have a greater awareness of the potential vulnerabilities than just using a checklist of questions, proposed by CVSS, which was created on the basis of experts who did not have the organisational context.

 

 7.5)  SSVC does not use numerical scores, I think this is an advantage because it eliminates a false sense of security, from the decision making process. Vulnerability management is complex, and stakeholders need to understand the risks, and not rely on a numerical scoring system.

 

 7.6) SSVC and Current Information Sources

 

The authors propose that decision points can also be informed by using vendor feeds of vulnerabilities, if a valid feed can be matched to an organisation.

 

 Summary

 

There are obvious drawbacks to CCVS as mentioned in this post, and my view is that it simplifies vulnerability management, and cannot be effective because it doesn’t take into account the broader context, and environmental factors, as well as the fact that each organisation is unique and there needs to be a method which is flexible enough to deal with each unique organisation.

 

It is not possible to have a ‘one size fits all’ method, CVSS is too rigid and is also misleading by providing a numerical representation, which is not based on ‘real data’.

 

SSVC attempts to capture the complexity of different organisations and focuses on the outcome of vulnerability management which is to support the decision making process in terms of which vulnerabilities to fix and where to focus resources. It does this by involving stakeholders, who are familiar with the organisation and using decision trees.

 

With industry 4.0 I think that CVSS will become even more ineffective in being able to manage vulnerabilities, because it doesn’t model the organisational context sufficiently.

Hi Jane,

That was indeed a very complete and in-depth overview of the topic. One question which comes to my mind reading our forums and the discussions on CVSS is how easy will it be for a complete industry to move away from CVSS to CCVS having witnessed already the sometimes how companies and vendors find it difficult to move between versions of CVSS itself. As Industry 4.0 is coming along together with IoT devices and other new technologies it becomes difficult to understand the possible impact on safety by looking at the individual metrics.

 

We are accustomed to look into scoring from a confidentiality, integrity and availability vector perspective. As Klidenist points out safety and the potential for loss of life is a real concern in Industry 4.0 technologies including the modern automotive industry. So as you mentioned not only CVSS is not able to model the organisational context sufficiently but it also fails to include metrics which are relevant for the technological context and the environment in which the technology operates. I would also argue whether it would be reasonable to expect CVSS to be able to cope as a metric with risks which extend beyond the topics of information technology, such as the potential for loss of life. Similar questions have been raised in the past with respect to SCADA systems and potential safety implication but without coming up with a scoring system for addressing such threats.

Hi Iason,

 

Thank you for your feedback on my post.

In terms of how easy it will be for the industry to move away from CVSS, it will be a ‘paradigm shift’, but I do believe that this is possible, because of the inherent flaws in CCVS (Spring et al, 2021). When I started to work on this analysis, I was not planning to propose that SSVC replaces CVSS, instead I was thinking that CVSS could incorporate aspects of SSVC, because it doesn’t make sense to ‘reinvent the wheel’, especially as CVSS is widely used throughout the industry. However after reading the paper ‘Prioritizing vulnerability response: A stakeholder-specific vulnerability categorization’ by Spring et al (2021), I realised that CVSS is fundamentally flawed. There have been similar paradigm shifts, such as the move from the waterfall development methodology to agile, which was actually a greater change for the technology industry, than moving from CVSS to a different framework like SSVC (Lynn, 2022).

I think that the driver which could make this shift happen, is if vulnerabilities which were not identified or prioritised by CVSS manifest themselves in significant incidents or exploits. More and more failings of CVSS will become apparent due to industry 4.0, because CVSS is very inward looking, it doesn’t look at the broader organisational context. I included some blogs in my analysis because they were from practitioners of CVSS, who were obviously frustrated by its’ failings. As an example, here is a quote from Ross, A (2019) :

‘our X-Force Red hackers have seen time and time again vulnerabilities with CVSS scores of 10 bumped to the top of the priority list even though the assets they could affect would only cause minimal impact to the business if they were compromised. High scores were also attributed to vulnerabilities even when criminals were not exploiting them in the wild at that time.’

The fact that CVSS is used by the US government and leveraged by payments processing, also means that if there is a major incident, which CVSS has not captured, due to a missed vulnerability, then there will be lot of focus on how to improve or replace CVSS.

An article explaining why a major consultancy is moving away from CVSS also backs up this view (Schwartz, 2021).

In terms of your comment that CVSS is not able to cope with risks, which extend beyond information technology, I would go further than this and state that CVSS is not a risk methodology, it just deals with vulnerabilities, which are different (Spring et al, 2021).
