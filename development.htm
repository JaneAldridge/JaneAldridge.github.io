Learning Development and Changed Actions

There were a number of cases, where upon reflection, I developed my thinking further during this course:

Example Task

Learning Development through Reflection

For the second collaborative discussion we were asked to look at the effectiveness of CVSS, after reading a paper by Spring et al (2021)

When I started this analysis, I was against proposing to replace CVSS, because CVSS is widely used.  I didn’t think it made sense to reinvent the wheel.  However, after reading another paper by Spring et al (2021) entitled ‘Prioritizing Vulnerability Response: A Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization (Version 2.0)’, my thinking changed because I felt that CVSS was fundamentally flawed and that a completely different methodology was required.

What we had learnt about industry 4.0 earlier in the module, also impacted my thinking, because industry 4.0 brings a number of new external supply chain risks, and CVSS is too inward looking.

 

For the final unit, we were asked to present on what we thought would be the biggest future trend in SRM over the next 5 years

I presented at the final seminar on software supply chain management being the biggest trend over the next 5 years. However, after hearing the other presentations during the seminar, which were focused on ML/AI, I realised that ML/AI can be used for predictive cyber risk analytics, and could reduce the challenges of the supply chain.

Post the seminar, I read a paper by Radanliev et al (2020) and then updated my original presentation to include how ML/AI can be used to address supply chain risk, by using real time predictive cyber risk analytics.

As part of this module, we have looked in depth at different risk methods, such as FAIR and CVSS, as well as quantitative methods such as Monte Carlo.

When I started this module I was of the opinion that every organisation could use the same standard risk methodology.  However after understanding the issues with using CVSS and after reading feedback on CVSS from cyber security SME’s, as well as looking at the complexity of a software supply chain, my thinking changed and I felt that each organisation is so different and faces so many different types of risks, that a framework is required which uses different risk tools for different scenarios.  The framework has to include quantitative methods, which provide standardization, and calculating risk probability is important from a commercial perspective. This wasn’t evident until I compared the different results from unit 6 and unit 11.
