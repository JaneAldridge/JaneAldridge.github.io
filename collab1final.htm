As you mention, industry 4.0 will result in job losses for unskilled labour. I think this is an important point and it has been estimated by McKinsey that around one fifth of the global workforce will be affected in a variety of industries, which is a huge impact (Frank, 2022).  

Another category of risk which you have highlighted are external behavioural risks, and this is where there needs to be a shift in thinking.  The majority of risks with Industry 4.0 are going to reside with the other partners and customers in the value chain, and there needs to be a way of categorising these external risks and having mitigations in place, and this is going to be challenging when dealing with an external entity. A report by ENSA (The European Union Agency for Network and Information Security) states that industry 4.0 actors should clarify regulatory liability amongst themselves (Malatras et al, 2019), carry out regular industry 4.0 supply chain risk assessments, rely on suppliers who products comply with recognised security standards and certification schemes, apply trust models instead of concrete technical security controls etc.. The report states that end to end security is a prerequisite for industry 4.0 to succeed, and I think this requires a new model for assessing risk.

What do the authors mean by the term ‘Industry 4.0’ – give two examples

The fourth industrial revolution was a term first used by Klaus Schwab, founder and executive chairman of the World Economic forum (Xu et al, 2018). It describes a collection of new disruptive technologies which will change industry and wider society.  These technologies include the internet of things, artificial intelligence, cloud infrastructure, big data/analytics, nano-technology, advanced robotics, sensors, blockchain, 3D printing, augmented reality, quantum computing and edge computing (Gravenhorst, 2022).  The huge impact of industry 4.0 is in bringing these new technologies together. When compared to previous industrial revolutions, the speed of the fourth industrial revolution is exponential. Schwab states that these changes are transforming entire systems of production, management and governance (Schwab, 2022).

An example of how these new disruptive technologies have come together to impact the manufacturing supply chain is described in a paper called ‘What does Industry 4.0 mean to Supply Chain?’ (Tjahjono et al, 2017).  Industry 4.0 has enabled networks of machines in smart factories to be able to autonomously exchange information with each other and control each other.  This is called a cyber-physical system, and means that the factory can operate autonomously.  Industry 4.0 impacts the whole supply chain, from the suppliers, manufacturers to the customers.  With IoT, big data and artificial intelligence machines can not only communicate with each other, and automate production lines but can also analyse and solve production issues, with minimal human involvement.  Machine failure has been a costly expense but now visual inspection of factory machines has been automated using cellular networks, machine learning and augmented reality technologies.  High resolution cameras connected to a private cellular network enable instant and accurate inspection of factory assets (Torres, 2022).  Autonomous mobile robots (AMR’s) combine AI and 5G’s ability to provide real time data transfer to enable the free movement of AMR’s around the factory floor.  Factories are also leveraging hundreds of sensors on the factory floor and cellular networks to improve efficiency and energy consumption (Torres, 2022). 

Another example is the ability to monitor equipment at ports.  Industry 4.0 has enabled connected equipment at ports to be monitored in real time for any issues, before a breakdown occurs (Diekmann, 2022).  A private cellular network enables port operators to be able to have wireless connectivity across the entire operation, on land, air and sea.  Wireless IoT monitoring systems with precise sensor technology can monitor for potential equipment failure before it happens.  25% of the cost of equipment damage in ports is due to inadequate maintenance (Diekmann, 2022).  Cellular connected sensors can continuously monitor the condition of critical assets such as cranes, AGV’s, stacks of containers, and take into consideration factors such as temperature and vibration.  The data from sensors is then analysed in real time, using the cloud, and AI technology to detect potential faults, which then reduces the number of breakdowns significantly.  In ports such as Rotterdam, driverless trucks are controlled with the help of sensors, and a computer which instructs each truck where to position storage containers.

Give two real-world examples of risks that fit into the authors categories      

One of the categories of risk proposed by the authors in the article ‘Risks of Digitalisation of Business models’ are data security risks, involving the security of data and information (Kovaite et al, 2019). 

Deloitte in their paper ‘Industry 4.0 and Cybersecurity: Managing risk in an age of connected production’ agree with the significant increase in risk which Industry 4.0 brings (Waslo et al, 2017).  Industry 4.0 brings connected platforms and devices together, with a digital supply network that captures data from across the value chain.  But the risk here is that data has to be shared across the supply chain or network. This poses a significant risk increase in terms of increased transparency of data and data sharing. As an example, by giving increased access to data, it increases the risk of cyber criminals being able to access data.  Also, competitors in the value chain may have accidental access to proprietary information such as pricing data.  These risks can be reduced by network segmentation, by encrypting data at rest and in transit, by having strong monitoring capabilities and a strong cyber security team. But organisations need to be aware of these increased risks, which come with industry 4.0, in order to address them. 

An example of a technical risk involving the cloud is what happened to Raychat in February 2021, when a cloud database configuration breach enabled cyber criminals access to 267 million usernames and other personal data, as a result a bot attack followed which erased the company’s data (Cybertalk, 2022).

One of the categories of risk cited in the paper are technical risks, In 2018 there was a GitHub attack, where a large botnet carried out the largest DDoS attack.  The attack took GitHub, the largest software development platform on the internet offline (Moes, 2021).

IoT devices increase the cyber risk significantly, in terms of a real world example in October 2016 there was a DDoS attack via Mirai malware where a virus infected IoT devices such as connected cameras and televisions and turned them into botnets (Waslo, 2017). 

Find another journal article that either supports or contradicts the points made in the cited study

Deloitte in their article ‘Managing risk in an age of connected production’ supports the points made in the cited study, to quote Deloitte ‘As industry moves to capture the business value that comes with Industry 4.0, the need to address the cyber risk landscape with a secure, vigilant and resilient response has likely never been greater’ (Waslo, 2017).

In the paper ‘The Fourth Industrial Revolution: Opportunities and Challenges’ the authors support the cited study and state that there is an increase in challenges such as cybersecurity, hacking, risk assessment and others (Xu, 2018).  This risk obviously increases when various devices are connected such as cell phones, cars, light switches, smart speakers and home security systems.
