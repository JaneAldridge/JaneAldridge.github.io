Summary Post

by Jane Aldridge - Friday, 16 September 2022, 9:59 PM
Number of replies: 0

After defining industry 4.0 in my initial post, I then provided two examples. The first was the impact on a manufacturing supply chain, where industry 4.0 had enabled networks of machines to be able to autonomously exchange information with each other, and the second example was where industry 4.0 had enabled connected equipment at ports to be monitored in real time for any issues.   

For real world examples of risks which fall into the categories mentioned in the paper, I described how industry 4.0 increases ‘data security risk’ because it means that data has to be shared across the supply chain.  For the technical risk category, I described the Raychat cybersecurity incident where a cloud database configuration breach enabled cyber criminals to access 267 million usernames, and also the 2018 GitHub attack, where a large botnet carried out the largest DDos attack.   

I referred to two papers in my initial post, which supported the points made in the study (‘Managing risk in an age of connected production’, and ‘The Fourth Revolution: Opportunities and Challenges’). 

In terms of peer responses I made the following points :

-       Industry 4.0 increases the risk of a regulatory issue, because supply chain data has to be shared externally, which could result in a data breach and a regulatory fine under GDPR

-       The majority of risk with industry 4.0 will reside with external partners and customers in the supply chain

From reading my peers comments, it became apparent that a new risk model was required for managing risk for industry 4.0, and this was supported by the ENISA report of 2019. This new risk model would need to focus on external end to end security risk, which could be challenging because companies will need to be transparent about their risks and controls.
